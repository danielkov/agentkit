THE SKERRING LIGHT

Marcus Halvarsen had served as the lighthouse keeper on Skerring Island
for thirty-one years before the autumn storm of 1947 swept his cottage
into the North Sea and erased every record of his tenure. For forty
years the wreckage was left where it lay, the salt-scoured beams
half-buried in the gravel beach, the great brass lens of the lamp lying
broken among the kelp. The light itself, a red iron tower set on the
western point, had been replaced in 1953 by an automatic beacon, and
the mainland villagers of Tordal preferred to forget that anyone had
ever lived out there alone. They remembered the wreck only when a
westerly storm reminded them of it, which was perhaps four times a
year, and even then only the older fishermen could be drawn into a
conversation about the keeper or the loss.

In the spring of 1987, Elena Brage, a researcher with the Maritime
Heritage Trust in Bergen, came to Tordal in pursuit of an old rumour:
that Halvarsen had kept a journal, and that the journal had not been
lost with the cottage but locked in a metal chest and buried somewhere
on the island. Elena had spent the previous eight months with the
surviving notebooks of two other Norwegian keepers, one from Lista and
one from Andenes, both of them unremarkable. She did not expect
Skerring to yield much. She was thirty-four, methodical, sceptical of
the romance that often attached itself to lighthouses in the popular
press, and she had brought with her a steel probe, a folding spade, a
camera, and a notebook of her own. She did not bring much faith.

The boat captain who agreed to take her was Tomas Lien, a wiry man of
sixty who had grown up running supplies to the keepers between 1939
and 1953. Tomas remembered Halvarsen as a quiet man who paid in
advance and asked for very little: kerosene, salted herring, a tin of
black tea that came once a year on the September run, a single bar of
laundry soap, and on three occasions an order of writing paper. He
remembered the storm too. He had been seventeen years old in October
of 1947 and he had been the one who, three days after the wind had
dropped, took his father's boat out to look for Halvarsen and found
only the foundation of the cottage and the cracked lens of the lamp.

They left Tordal harbour on the morning of the seventh of May, 1987,
in a green-painted launch named the Storfugl. The crossing took four
hours. Skerring was a low island shaped like a curled fist, ringed on
its eastern side by a beach of black gravel and on its western side by
sheer cliffs against which the lighthouse stood. The automatic beacon
flashed every six seconds, mechanical and indifferent, and the only
sound apart from the surf was the periodic clatter of a loose panel on
the service door. Elena set up her camp on the lee side of the
foundation, a square of mortared stone half-claimed by lichen, and
Tomas walked the perimeter, marking with red flags any spot where the
ground sounded hollow under the heel of his boot.

For three days they probed the gravel and the peat and found nothing
but the iron hoops of an old herring barrel, two corroded shillings
dated 1922, and the ribs of a seal that had died a winter or two
before. The weather held: cold, bright, with a steady wind from the
north that smelled of ice and made the work tiring. On the second
evening Tomas told Elena the only story about Halvarsen he had ever
been told as a boy, which was that the keeper had once had a wife,
named Astrid, and that she had died in childbirth in the winter of
1919 and was buried in the cemetery at Tordal. He had not remarried,
had not taken leave, had not, as far as Tomas's father could recall,
spoken her name aloud after the funeral. Elena wrote this down. She
was beginning to understand that the journal, if it existed, would
not be a register of weather and supply runs.

On the fourth morning, Tomas, who had been growing impatient, walked
north along the beach to a small cove that he remembered Halvarsen
using to gut fish. The cove was sheltered by two basalt outcrops, and
the gravel there was finer and paler than elsewhere on the island.
Tomas drove the steel probe into the ground at the base of the
southern outcrop and felt it strike something solid two and a half
feet down. He called out once and Elena came at a run.

The chest, when they uncovered it, was a black iron strongbox of the
sort sold by Hagstrom and Company of Oslo in the 1930s, fitted with a
brass padlock and wrapped in two layers of tarred sailcloth. The
cloth was largely intact, the iron only lightly rusted. The lock
yielded to a chisel. Inside, packed in a sealed tobacco tin to keep
out the damp, was a leather-bound journal of one hundred and
forty-two pages, written in Halvarsen's small upright hand, beginning
in October of 1916 and ending on the eighteenth of October, 1947 --
six days before the storm. Beneath the journal lay a smaller bundle
wrapped in oilcloth, which Elena did not open until later that
evening: a single photograph of a young woman with dark hair and a
serious mouth, and a folded sheet of paper bearing, in pencil, three
lines of poetry that Elena did not recognise.

Elena read the journal twice on the boat back to Tordal and a third
time that night in her room at the Tordal hostel. The early entries
were what one might expect: weather, supply runs, the behaviour of
the lamp, complaints about the price of kerosene. From 1923 onward,
however, the journal began to record something else. Halvarsen had
observed, in nine separate winters between 1923 and 1946, a faint
green light moving on the water to the north-west of the island,
always between the hours of two and four in the morning, always in
the week of the new moon, and always -- this is what struck him --
moving against the prevailing current. He had at first assumed it
was a phosphorescent slick of some kind, but the pattern was too
regular. Beginning in 1928, he had kept careful notes: bearing,
duration, weather, his own state of fatigue. By 1939 he had become
convinced that the light was the work of a vessel running without
lamps, and that this vessel was making landfall on a stretch of coast
about eleven nautical miles to the north-west, near a sheltered bay
called Refsvika.

The middle pages of the journal contained a hand-drawn chart of the
bay, a table of dates with bearings to two decimal places, and a
careful cipher in which Halvarsen had recorded a name -- Holger Kvist
-- and a date, the third of March, 1942. Holger Kvist, Elena would
later confirm through the wartime archives in Oslo, had been a known
operative of the Norwegian resistance who had organised the
evacuation of nineteen Allied airmen out of the western coast between
1941 and 1944, and whose escape route had never been satisfactorily
reconstructed. The cipher entry in Halvarsen's journal was the only
direct evidence ever recovered that the route had passed through
Refsvika.

Halvarsen had told no one. He had paid for kerosene in advance and
asked for tea once a year and tended his lamp and watched the green
light cross the water nine winters in a row, and when the war was
over he had continued to keep his journal and his lamp until the
storm of October 1947. There was, in the entries between 1942 and
1945, a tone of attention that was new to the document, a sense of a
man keeping a witness he could not share. He recorded, on the night
of the third of March, 1942, that the light had passed within four
hundred metres of his own beach for the first time, that he had not
shown his lamp, and that at four-twenty in the morning he had heard,
faintly above the wind, the sound of a man laughing.

In the entries from 1946 there is, finally, a softening. Halvarsen
writes about the gulls. He writes about a particular evening light
that fell across the gravel for half an hour each May. He writes,
once, about Astrid, by name, in a sentence that breaks off without a
verb. The final entry, dated the eighteenth of October, 1947, reads
in full: "Wind rising from the south-south-west. Glass falling. Have
moved the chest to the cove. If the cottage goes, the light at least
will hold."

The light had held. The cottage had not. Halvarsen's body was never
recovered, and the search was abandoned after eleven days.

Elena published the journal in 1989, in a small edition with a
foreword by the Maritime Heritage Trust and an annotated
transcription of the cipher. The book sold eight hundred copies in
its first printing and was reviewed in three Norwegian newspapers and
one British naval history journal. Holger Kvist, who was
eighty-one and living in Stavanger, attended the launch in Bergen and
spoke for seven minutes about a keeper he had never met, whose lamp
he had used as a navigation reference on six crossings, and whose
name he had never until that evening known. He thanked Elena, in
Norwegian and then in English, and then sat down and did not speak
for the rest of the evening. Tomas Lien did not attend; he was at
sea, on the September run to a smaller island further north, where
he had agreed to deliver kerosene, salted herring, and a single tin
of black tea to a keeper who had been there only two years and who
was, by all accounts, a quiet man.

A note on the photograph: it was identified, by a cousin of Astrid
Halvarsen still living in Tordal at the time of the publication, as
having been taken at the parish church on the morning of the
Halvarsens' wedding in May of 1916. Marcus would have been
twenty-three. Astrid was twenty-one. The photograph is undated and
unsigned, and it had been kept, it seems, in the chest with the
journal for the entire span of his keepership, removed only, perhaps,
on certain evenings, and replaced again before the storm. The three
lines of poetry on the folded sheet were eventually traced to a minor
nineteenth-century Danish poet named Anders Vedel Holm. They
describe a road that runs along the sea at night, and a light that
is steady, and a hand that is not. Elena chose not to print the
poem in the published edition, on the grounds that to do so would be
to interpret the keeper rather than to record him, and she had
already done enough of the former. The poem and the photograph are
held today in the archives of the Maritime Heritage Trust in Bergen,
catalogued as accession SK-1987-04, available to researchers on
written application, and rarely consulted.

Marcus Halvarsen had not been born to the sea. His father, Johann
Halvarsen, was a clerk in the customs house at Bergen who in 1893
moved his family inland to the small farming village of Voss, where
Johann had inherited a half share of his uncle's pasture. The boy
Marcus grew up among cattle and apple trees and the granite walls of
the old church and saw the sea only twice before his eighteenth
year: once on a school excursion in 1903, and once in the summer of
1908, when his mother Ingrid, in declining health, was taken by
train to Stavanger to consult a specialist who could not help her.
Ingrid Halvarsen died in the Stavanger hospital on a Tuesday morning
in August of that year, and on the train back to Voss her son sat
with his face to the window for nine hours, watching first the
fjords and then the green Hardanger valleys, and writing nothing in
the small black notebook he had carried with him from school. The
notebook survives. Its pages from that summer are blank except for
the single word "salt" written in pencil on the inside back cover.

After his mother's death the family did not prosper. Johann
Halvarsen, who had never reconciled himself to country life, drank
quietly and steadily and sold off his half of the pasture in 1911 to
a cousin from Aurland for less than it was worth. The proceeds went
mostly to the settlement of debts that the village had not known
existed. In the spring of 1912, Marcus, then nineteen, walked to
Bergen along the coast road and apprenticed himself to the
Norwegian Lighthouse Authority as a junior keeper. The decision was
not romantic. The Authority paid a small wage in advance, provided
food and lodging at the training station on Solund, and required no
prior experience beyond literacy and the ability to operate a small
boat. Marcus had read about lighthouses in a textbook at school. He
liked the idea of working alone.

He spent two years at Solund learning the maintenance of clockwork
lenses, the trimming of wicks, the care of mercury baths, and the
keeping of the daily log. The senior keeper there, an older man
called Ovesen who had served on six different stations across forty
years, took to him slowly and then, eventually, with the protective
attention an unsentimental man can sometimes give to a young one. It
was Ovesen who first told Marcus about Skerring, which had become
vacant in the spring of 1914 after the previous keeper, a man named
Lillebo, had died there of a coronary at the age of forty-nine.
Skerring was difficult to staff. It was remote, the cottage was old
and damp, and the supply runs from Tordal were unreliable in winter.
Most of the Authority's keepers preferred the southern stations,
which were closer to towns and to other keepers. Ovesen suggested
that a young man with no family attachments and an interest in
solitude might find Skerring suitable. Marcus did not visit it
before accepting the post. He took the steamer from Bergen to
Trondheim in October of 1915, transferred to a coastal packet, and
arrived in Tordal on the eighth of November to find that the
outgoing keeper, a man named Tellef who had been holding the post on
a temporary basis, had already left for the mainland and that the
lamp had been unattended for three nights.

Astrid Berg, who would become his wife the following May, was the
schoolteacher in Tordal that winter. She was twenty years old, the
daughter of a Tordal fisherman, and had recently completed her
training at a teachers' college in Bergen. The school had only
eleven pupils, the eldest of whom was fourteen. Astrid lived in two
rooms above the post office. Marcus, when he came in to Tordal for
his quarterly supplies, would stop at the schoolhouse to deliver, on
behalf of an aunt, a small parcel of cured ham and a tin of
peppermint sweets, and on the third such delivery, in February of
1916, he asked Astrid if she would be willing to walk with him on
the harbour wall. She said yes. They walked for an hour on a clear
cold afternoon and discussed, of all things, the geology of the
Voss valley and the lighting strategy of the Skerring lamp. Astrid
later wrote, in a letter to her sister in Lillehammer, that she had
not laughed so much in nine months. They were engaged in March and
married in May, in the small parish church of Tordal, before
nineteen people. The bride wore a dress that had belonged to her
mother. The groom borrowed a black coat from the post office
manager. After the wedding, Astrid moved to Skerring with her
husband and a small wooden trunk containing two sets of clothing,
six books, a teapot, and the photograph that would later be found
in the chest.

The first three years on Skerring were the happiest period of
Marcus's life. The journal entries from 1916 to early 1919 are the
most informal in the entire document: the handwriting is looser, the
margins are filled with small drawings of birds and the occasional
sketch of Astrid bent over a stove or reading by lamplight. There is
a note in March of 1917 about a pair of eider ducks that had begun
to nest behind the cistern. There is, in October of 1918, a careful
list of the books Astrid had finished that summer, with a small star
beside the ones Marcus intended to read next. There is, in
February 1919, a passage about the way snow accumulated on the
windward side of the lamp tower and how the two of them went out
together in the early afternoon, before the dark, to clear it. The
passage ends with the sentence: "She is a better climber than I am,
which is one of many things she has not pointed out."

Astrid died on the seventeenth of December, 1919. The pregnancy had
been difficult since October. Tomas Lien's father, then a young man
of twenty-two, had been the one to make the winter run that brought
the midwife from Tordal in a small open boat through weather the
Authority's standing instructions would not have permitted. The
midwife arrived three hours too late. The child, a boy, was
stillborn. Astrid herself died of haemorrhage at four in the morning
in the bedroom of the cottage, with Marcus beside her, holding her
hand. The body was taken back to Tordal on the same boat, in
weather that had begun to clear. She was buried in the parish
cemetery on the twenty-second of December, in a brief service
attended by her father, two of her sisters, the schoolchildren of
Tordal, and Marcus, who returned alone to Skerring that same
evening.

The journal does not record her death directly. There is a blank
page for the seventeenth, the eighteenth, and the nineteenth of
December. The entry for the twentieth reads, in full: "Lamp
trimmed. Glass clean. Wind east, four to five. Will continue."

He continued for twenty-eight more years.

The middle years of Halvarsen's tenure -- the period from 1920 to
roughly 1935 -- are the most uniform in the journal. The entries
from this stretch read as if written by a man who has decided
deliberately to write nothing personal. The weather is recorded.
The fuel deliveries are recorded. The condition of the lamp and its
clockwork mechanism is recorded. The supply runs are recorded. The
visits from Authority inspectors -- there were eleven of them
between 1920 and 1935 -- are recorded with the inspector's name, the
date of arrival, and the date of departure, and nothing else. There
are no drawings. There are no margins. The handwriting itself
becomes smaller and more uniform, as if Halvarsen were practising a
steadiness of hand that the words on their own could not provide.
And yet within this uniformity certain entries stand out, in
retrospect, for their attention to particular natural phenomena: a
note in May of 1923 on the unusual behaviour of a particular
guillemot colony on the eastern cliffs; a longer note in August of
1925 on the colour of the sea after a thunderstorm; a careful
description, in October of 1927, of an aurora that lasted for three
nights and was visible even at the southern horizon. These entries
are the bridge between the first private years and the later, more
focused observations. Halvarsen had not stopped looking. He had
only stopped looking inward.

The first sighting of the green light, on the night of the eleventh
of January, 1923, occupies most of a single journal page. Halvarsen
records that he had been awake at two in the morning because the
clockwork in the lamp tower had been running a quarter-second slow
all evening and he had finally gone up at quarter past one to
adjust the regulator. He describes the weather: clear, no moon, a
steady wind from the north-north-west at perhaps three knots, the
sea calm. He went out onto the small balcony that ran around the
lamp room to check the windward side of the glass. As he was
turning to go back inside, he saw, on the water about two
nautical miles to the north-west, a faint green glow that lasted, he
estimated, between forty and fifty seconds, and that moved -- and
this is the detail he underlines -- against the prevailing tidal
current at an apparent speed of perhaps four knots. He noted the
bearing. He went back to bed. In the morning he wrote down what he
had seen and added the words: "Phosphorescent slick? But the speed
is wrong."

He saw the light again on the seventh of February, 1923, and on the
twelfth of March, 1923, both times during the new moon week, both
times in the same general direction, both times moving against the
current. After the third sighting he began to keep a separate page
in the back of his journal where he tabulated, for each occurrence,
the date, the bearing, the duration, the wind, the sea state, the
moon phase, and his own state of fatigue. The table grew slowly. By
the end of 1925 it had eight entries. By the end of 1928 it had
fourteen. By the end of 1930 it had nineteen. The entries from 1931
through 1937 are sparser -- only nine in seven years -- and
Halvarsen, in a marginal note from 1936, suggests two possible
reasons: either the source had become more cautious, or his own
sleep schedule, increasingly disrupted by the demands of the lamp,
had caused him to miss occurrences.

What changed in 1939 was that Halvarsen began to listen as well as
look. He had had, that summer, a brief visit from a wartime
inspector named Gjerde, who had asked, in passing and over coffee,
whether Halvarsen had seen any unusual maritime activity in his
sector. Halvarsen had said no. After Gjerde left, Halvarsen sat for
some time at his kitchen table, looking out at the sea, and then
went to the journal and began, that evening, a new section in which
he attempted to reconstruct, from his earlier entries, every
sighting he had recorded between 1923 and 1939. The reconstruction
took him most of a winter. By March of 1940 he had produced a
hand-drawn chart of the bearings, a list of dates aligned with the
new moon calendar, a tentative estimate of the vessel's likely
landfall, and a single short paragraph, in slightly larger
handwriting than the rest of the document, in which he wrote: "I
believe it is people. I believe they are running between the
mainland and a place I have not seen. I will say nothing of this to
the inspector when he comes again."

The 1942 close pass is the most detailed entry in the journal. It
runs to four pages and was clearly composed over several days, with
emendations in different inks. Halvarsen records that on the night
of the third of March he was already awake when the light appeared
-- he had been writing a letter, a fact he does not elaborate on --
and that he saw the light first at one-fifty in the morning, on the
expected bearing, but moving more slowly than usual and on a course
that brought it gradually closer to Skerring than any prior
occurrence. He estimates the closest point of approach as four
hundred metres off the western point of the island, well inside the
zone in which the rotating beam of the Skerring lamp would normally
have illuminated any vessel. Halvarsen, who had been about to
relight the lamp after the routine half-past-one trim, deliberately
did not. He stood in the dark on the balcony, his eyes adjusting,
and watched the light for the full duration of the close pass --
twenty-three minutes by his own count -- and then for a further
half-hour as it receded toward Refsvika. At four-twenty he heard,
faintly, between gusts, a single human sound: a man laughing. He
records this in three places in the entry, each time with the same
phrase: "the sound of a man laughing." He does not comment on it
further. He records the time at which he relit the lamp: five-oh-
seven. He notes that he did not sleep again that night.

The cipher entry naming Holger Kvist is dated the next morning, the
fourth of March, 1942. It occupies a single line in the back of the
journal, written in a substitution cipher that Halvarsen had begun
using for sensitive entries in 1940 and that Elena would, in 1988,
identify as a simple shift cipher with a key word taken from the
opening of the Anders Vedel Holm poem. Decoded, the entry reads:
"Holger Kvist. Refsvika. Nineteen so far." The number is significant.
By the time of his second visit to Bergen, in 1989, Holger Kvist had
confirmed that nineteen Allied airmen had passed through his
Refsvika route between October of 1941 and March of 1942. After
March of 1942 the route was used twice more, in May and September of
1943, both times for resistance personnel rather than airmen, and
both times without the green light, as a result of a directive
issued by the Norwegian command in London after a near-interception
in April. Halvarsen, on Skerring, did not know any of this. He
recorded, in May and September of 1943, only that the light had not
appeared on the expected nights and that he had felt a kind of
uncertainty he had not previously had words for.

The postwar entries, from 1945 through 1947, are the most varied of
the journal. There is a long entry in June of 1945 in which
Halvarsen attempts a kind of summary: he writes about the lamp,
about the routine, about the way certain afternoon light fell across
the gravel, about a guillemot colony that had recently relocated.
He does not write about the war. He does not mention Holger Kvist.
He does not refer to the cipher. The pages from this period are
softer in tone than anything since 1919, and several of them contain
small marginal drawings -- the first since the early years -- of
ducks, of clouds, of the silhouette of the lamp tower against
evening sky. There is, in May of 1946, the entry that breaks off
mid-sentence at Astrid's name: "When Astrid would have" -- and
nothing more. The next day's entry begins, in a different ink, with
a description of the morning weather.

The October 1947 storm had been forecast for three days. The
Authority had issued a general advisory on the fifteenth, warning
keepers along the western coast to secure outbuildings and to
expect winds of force eight or higher. Halvarsen's entries from the
fifteenth, sixteenth, and seventeenth describe a methodical
preparation: the boat was hauled up onto blocks, the windward
shutters were reinforced, the spare wicks and the kerosene reserve
were moved into the lamp tower itself, where the masonry was
strongest. On the eighteenth, his last entry, he notes the
falling glass, the rising wind, and the relocation of the chest to
the cove. The journal does not survive the night. The chest does.
The cottage and the man do not. The Authority's reconstruction
later concluded, on the basis of debris distribution, that the
cottage roof failed at some time between two and three in the
morning of the nineteenth, that the western wall went shortly
afterwards, and that whatever remained of the structure was carried
into the sea by the second high tide of the storm, at approximately
five in the morning. The lamp itself, set on bedrock and built of
mortared stone, remained standing throughout, though its lens was
shattered by airborne debris and the clockwork mechanism was found,
five days later, by Tomas Lien, in the gravel of the eastern beach,
seventy metres from where it had been installed.

Elena Brage's path to Skerring was indirect. She had grown up in
Bergen, had read history and Norwegian literature at the university
there, and had joined the Maritime Heritage Trust in 1981 as a
junior cataloguer. Her early work was administrative: she
processed donations, wrote condition reports, and corresponded with
descendants of merchant seamen who had material to deposit. Her
first independent research project was a small monograph on the
Authority's pension scheme between 1880 and 1920, which she
published in the Trust's quarterly bulletin in 1984 and which is
still considered the standard reference on its subject. Her second
project, a longer study of three nineteenth-century coastal
beacons, was completed in 1985. By 1986 she had begun to specialise
in keepers' personal records, a category that the Trust had until
then largely ignored, and she had located and transcribed the
notebooks of the two earlier keepers -- one from Lista, one from
Andenes -- whose surviving material had been deposited in the Trust
archives in 1962 and 1971 respectively. Both notebooks were
unremarkable: weather, supply runs, occasional remarks about
visiting inspectors. Elena had nearly concluded that the genre was
inherently dull when, in October of 1986, she came across a single
sentence in an oral history conducted in 1973 with a retired Tordal
fisherman named Sverre Lid. Lid, then eighty-four, had said: "There
was a keeper at Skerring who kept his own book. The boy who buried
the chest with him said so."

Sverre Lid had died in 1979. The boy he referred to was, presumably,
Tomas Lien, who had been seventeen in 1947. Elena traced Tomas to
Tordal in early 1987 by writing to the local harbour authority. He
agreed to take her to the island. He did not, however, claim to
remember any chest. He remembered, he said, only what everyone in
Tordal remembered: that the cottage had been destroyed and that
Halvarsen had been lost. The expedition of May 1987 was, on
Tomas's part, undertaken largely as a courtesy. He did not expect
to find anything. He had not intended to volunteer the cove
location until the third evening, when the thoroughness of Elena's
search had, he later told her, made him reconsider his own
recollections.

The launch of the published edition, in November of 1989, was held
in the Trust's lecture room in Bergen on a Wednesday evening. There
were thirty-eight people in attendance, including Elena's parents,
six members of the Trust's executive committee, two journalists, an
elderly man from the Norwegian Resistance Museum, three members of
the Halvarsen family from Voss, and Holger Kvist, who travelled
from Stavanger and who, at the conclusion of his short remarks,
presented Elena with a small wooden navigation triangle that he had,
he said, used on the second of his Refsvika crossings. Tomas Lien
was at sea. The Halvarsen relatives, none of whom had known Marcus
personally, asked thoughtful questions about Astrid and listened
attentively to the answers. The journalists wrote competent
articles for their Sunday editions. The elderly man from the
Resistance Museum sat in the front row throughout the proceedings
and at the end approached Elena to say, only, "Thank you for the
care," and then left without giving his name.

The book has remained in print, in two further small editions, for
the thirty-six years since. Elena Brage retired from the Trust in
2018 and now lives in Voss, where she is writing a longer history of
the Norwegian coastal stations between the wars. Tomas Lien died in
2003 and is buried in the Tordal cemetery, near Astrid. Holger
Kvist died in 1994. The Skerring lamp, automatic since 1953, was
decommissioned in 2011 and replaced by a satellite-monitored beacon
of the kind now standard on uninhabited stations. The iron tower
still stands. The foundation of the cottage is still visible at low
tide. The cove where the chest was buried is still there, sheltered
by its two basalt outcrops, and, on a quiet day in spring, very
beautiful.

What follows is a selection of journal entries, transcribed from the
recovered original, intended to give the reader a sense of the
keeper's voice and of the texture of the document as a whole. The
entries are arranged chronologically. Editorial brackets indicate
illegible passages or obvious abbreviations expanded for clarity.
Halvarsen's spelling has been preserved except where strict
preservation would impede comprehension. The originals are held at
the Maritime Heritage Trust archive under accession SK-1987-04.

12 October 1916. Took up the post officially today. The previous
keeper, Tellef, had departed earlier than instructed and the lamp
had not been lit for three nights, a circumstance for which I have
written a brief report to the Authority and will hand to the
inspector at the next quarterly inspection. The cottage is in fair
condition though the southern window has settled and admits a small
amount of water in heavy rain. Will repair before winter. Astrid
has taken stock of the kitchen and found seven pounds of flour, a
half tin of lard, no salt, and a quantity of tea that I cannot
identify by smell. Have made list for Tordal run.

3 December 1916. First proper storm of the season. Force seven from
the south-west, lasting fourteen hours. Lamp held without
interruption. Astrid baked bread during the worst of it and we ate
it warm at midnight with the wind shaking the door. She said it
was the first storm she had ever properly enjoyed. I did not say
that it was, in a small way, the first storm I had ever properly
enjoyed either.

22 March 1917. The eider ducks have returned. They are nesting in
the same spot as last year, behind the cistern, in a small
depression that is sheltered from the prevailing wind. I will not
disturb them. Astrid is making a list of every bird species we have
seen this winter, partly for her own interest and partly, I
suspect, to teach me their names, which I do not know.

8 May 1917. First anniversary of the wedding. Walked together to
the eastern point in the morning. Found two stranded jellyfish and
one piece of green glass that Astrid says is from a Dutch ship. She
has put it on the windowsill. The day continued clear until late
afternoon when a fog came in from the south. Lamp lit at the usual
time. Quiet evening.

7 February 1918. Inspector Borg arrived two days late owing to ice
in Tordal harbour. Stayed one night. Found the lamp and the
auxiliary equipment in good order. Asked, as he always does,
whether anything unusual had been observed in the sector. I said
no. Astrid served him cabbage soup and he ate it without comment
and left in the morning. He is not a man for conversation but he is
fair.

12 December 1918. The boy in Astrid's belly kicks now. She felt it
first in October and we had been watching for it ever since. She
took my hand and put it on her side and we stood like that for some
minutes in the kitchen, neither of us saying anything. The lamp was
running quietly. The wind had dropped. I think I will remember this
evening for as long as I live.

20 December 1919. Lamp trimmed. Glass clean. Wind east, four to
five. Will continue.

5 January 1920. The lamp clockwork required a full disassembly and
cleaning today. The mercury bath has been losing pressure for some
weeks and on inspection I found a small hairline crack in the
secondary reservoir. Will request replacement at next inspection.
Have re-machined the regulator escape lever using the hand lathe.
The tower is cold but the work is steady.

18 March 1921. Tomas Lien -- the elder, the father of the boy who
will later succeed him -- made the supply run today in difficult
weather. He is a careful seaman and I have come to rely on him. He
brought, in addition to the supplies, a folded letter from my
father in Voss, my first letter from him in nine years. I read it
and put it in the drawer with the others. I will not write back.

11 January 1923. Saw, this evening, what I take to be a
phosphorescent slick on the water about two nautical miles north-
west of the island. The colour was greenish, the duration perhaps
forty seconds, and the apparent direction of motion was against the
current. The wind was steady from the north-north-west. There was
no moon. I have noted the bearing. Phosphorescence does not move
against current; perhaps it was a school of small fish. Will watch.

7 February 1923. Saw the green light again on roughly the same
bearing, again moving against the current, again for perhaps fifty
seconds. Tonight is also a new moon. Have begun a separate page in
the back of this journal for the recording of these occurrences.

12 March 1923. Third occurrence. Same bearing. Same direction.
Same phase of the moon. The pattern is too regular for any natural
cause I can identify. I will continue to record but I will, for
the moment, refrain from speculating in this journal.

18 August 1925. Inspector Marker arrived. Stayed two days. The
inspector is a young man, recently transferred from the Trondheim
office, and he asked many questions about the operation of the
lamp. I answered him honestly and at some length. He noted, in
passing, that I had unused leave accumulated and that I should
consider taking some of it. I said that I would. I will not.

3 November 1927. The aurora that has been visible for three nights
running has ceased. I have made a careful note of the colour, the
duration, and the apparent altitude in each case. The colour
varied from a pale green to a deep magenta. The altitude appeared
to extend, on the second night, to within ten degrees of the
zenith, which is unusual. I do not know what to make of these
observations. They are perhaps simply beautiful.

14 February 1929. The Authority has issued a circular notifying all
keepers of forthcoming changes to the lighting strategy. The
Skerring lamp is to be converted from kerosene to electric within
the next decade, contingent on the laying of an undersea cable from
Tordal. I will believe this when the cable arrives. In the meantime
I will continue to trim the wicks and clean the chimney glass and
order kerosene in the quantities I have always ordered.

22 December 1932. Christmas alone. The supply run was late by three
days due to a storm. Tomas Lien the elder brought a small packet of
Christmas biscuits made by his wife. They were good. The cottage is
warm. The lamp is steady. I am, by some measures, content. By other
measures I am not. I have stopped trying to reconcile the
measures.

7 March 1934. A fishing boat from Tordal, the Havsnja, ran aground
on the rocks at the eastern point in heavy fog. The crew of three
were able to reach the cottage and remained for two days while
their boat was salvaged. They were Eyvind Strom, Per Vatne, and a
young man named Erik whose surname I did not catch. They ate at the
table I had built for Astrid. They told stories about the Tordal
harbour and about a recent argument between two of the older
fishermen over the price of cod. They were courteous and grateful.
When they left, they thanked me with a kind of formality that was
out of proportion to the hospitality I had offered, and I realized,
after they had gone, that I had not heard the sound of three voices
in the cottage at the same time since 1919.

10 May 1936. Astrid would have been thirty-eight today. I lit the
lamp at seven-eighteen. The wind was from the south-west at three
knots. The sea was calm. I trimmed the wicks and cleaned the
chimney glass and made tea. The eider ducks have returned for the
twentieth consecutive year and are nesting in the same spot as
always. I do not know how long they live. I should ask Astrid -- I
should have asked Astrid -- I have been doing this for a long time
now and I do not always remember to keep my tenses straight.

29 September 1939. Inspector Gjerde, replacing Marker, arrived for
the autumn inspection. He is a serious man of perhaps forty-five
who served in the merchant marine before joining the Authority. He
inspected the lamp and the cottage thoroughly and asked, in
passing, whether I had observed any unusual maritime activity in
the sector. I said no. After he left I sat for a long time looking
at the sea. Tonight I have begun a reconstruction, from my earlier
entries, of every sighting of the green light I have recorded since
1923. The reconstruction will take me some time. I will not mention
it to anyone.

3 March 1942. The light passed the western point at four hundred
metres. I had been writing a letter and was already awake. I did
not relight the lamp at half-past one. I stood on the balcony in
the dark from one-fifty until two-thirteen, watching, and then for
a further half-hour as the light receded toward Refsvika. At four-
twenty I heard, faintly, the sound of a man laughing. I do not know
whose laughter it was. I record this here for completeness. The
lamp was relit at five-oh-seven. I did not sleep.

[Cipher entry, 4 March 1942: "Holger Kvist. Refsvika. Nineteen so
far."]

27 May 1943. The expected occurrence did not occur. I watched from
half-past one until four. The sea was calm and the moon was new and
the conditions were as favourable as they had ever been. The light
did not appear. I do not know what this means. It may mean that the
route has been abandoned. It may mean that the route has changed. It
may mean something else. I have nothing to compare it against.

12 September 1944. Inspector Gjerde, on what would prove to be his
final inspection of Skerring before his transfer to a desk position
in Bergen, asked me again whether I had observed any unusual
maritime activity in the sector. I said no. He looked at me for a
moment and then said, quietly, that he was glad to hear it, and we
spoke of other things.

8 May 1945. Peace declared. I lit the lamp at the usual time. I
made tea. I sat at the kitchen table for an hour. The journal will
not record what I felt. I do not have the words for it.

14 June 1945. Began the long entry I have intended to begin for
some weeks. Will not include it in this transcription, except to
note here that it concerns the eider ducks, the afternoon light
across the gravel, the relocation of the guillemot colony to the
southern cliffs, and a description of the way the lamp's reflection
falls on the surface of the cistern at certain times of the year.
It does not concern the war.

The publication of the journal in 1989 generated correspondence
that arrived at the Trust's offices for several months. Most of it
was from readers wishing to express their appreciation. A smaller
number of letters came from descendants of resistance operatives
who had read the cipher entry and wished to know whether their
relatives were among the nineteen airmen referenced. Elena
responded to each letter individually, and where she could trace a
connection she did so, and where she could not she said so. A
subset of the letters came from amateur cryptographers who had
been intrigued by the substitution cipher and who proposed
alternative readings, all of which Elena considered and, in each
case, rejected, on the grounds that the keyword from the Holm poem
could not plausibly be coincidental given Halvarsen's documented
ownership of the poem.

One letter, dated the third of February, 1990, came from a woman in
Trondheim named Inger Stromme, who identified herself as a
great-niece of Astrid Berg. She wrote to say that her family had
preserved a small bundle of Astrid's letters from the Skerring
years -- nine letters in all, written between 1916 and 1919 -- and
that they would like, if Elena was interested, to deposit them with
the Trust. Elena visited Trondheim in March of that year and read
the letters in Inger Stromme's living room over the course of a
single afternoon. The letters were addressed to Astrid's sister
Karen in Lillehammer and described, in clear and unsentimental
prose, the daily life of the cottage, the personality of Marcus
Halvarsen, the behaviour of the eider ducks, the books that were
being read aloud in the evenings, and the slow approach of the
pregnancy that would, in December of 1919, end her life. Elena
brought the letters back to Bergen and they were catalogued, in
1991, under accession SK-1990-12. They are, with the journal and
the photograph and the poem, the most consulted holdings in the
Skerring collection. Astrid Halvarsen's voice, preserved by an
accident of family piety, speaks across the seventy years between
her death and the discovery of her husband's chest in tones that
are companionable, observant, a little dry, and entirely her own.

A footnote, finally, on the Anders Vedel Holm poem. Holm, who
lived from 1841 to 1893, published only two collections of verse in
his lifetime, both with a small Copenhagen press, and was largely
forgotten by the time of his death. The three lines found in
Halvarsen's chest are from a poem entitled "Vejen ved Havet" (The
Road by the Sea), originally published in Holm's second collection,
'Aftenstykker' (Evening Pieces), Copenhagen, 1881. The full poem
is twenty-two lines. The three lines Halvarsen had transcribed are
the eighth, the ninth, and the tenth, and read, in approximate
English: "There is a road that runs along the sea at night. The
light upon it is steady. The hand that holds the light is not." It
is not known when, or from whom, Halvarsen had obtained the poem.
A small pencil annotation in his hand, on the reverse of the folded
sheet, contains the date "May 1916" and the initials "A.B." -- the
maiden initials of Astrid Berg.

The lighthouse itself, a structure of cast iron set on a granite
plinth and rising eleven metres above the western point of the
island, was constructed in 1879 by the Bergen firm Haaland and
Sons under contract to the Norwegian Lighthouse Authority. The
original specification called for a fixed white light of sixth
order, visible at a nominal range of fourteen nautical miles in
clear weather. In 1903 the lamp was upgraded to a fourth-order
revolving Fresnel lens with a characteristic of one flash every six
seconds, white, with a horizontal arc of three hundred and sixty
degrees minus a five-degree obscuration on the bearing of the
mainland to prevent confusion with shore lights. The 1903
installation was the lens that broke during the storm of October
1947. A replacement of the same specification was installed in
1953 when the station was converted to automatic operation, and
that replacement remained in service until the 2011 decommissioning.
The original 1879 plinth and tower remain in place. The cast iron
has been repainted, on the standard six-year cycle, twenty-two
times since the station was first commissioned.

The Skerring station was one of forty-three coastal lighthouses
constructed along the Norwegian western seaboard between 1855 and
1895, of which twenty-nine were still operational, in either
manned or automatic mode, at the time of Halvarsen's death in
1947. By the time of Elena's research in 1987, that number had
fallen to seventeen. By 2018 it had fallen to four. The Maritime
Heritage Trust, founded in 1971 with a small grant from the Norwegian
Ministry of Culture, has documented the operational history of every
station on the western coast and maintains, at its Bergen offices, a
public reading room in which the surviving records, photographs,
correspondence, and ephemera of the Norwegian lighthouse service may
be consulted by researchers, historians, descendants, and any member
of the public who has expressed an interest. The reading room is open
on Tuesdays, Wednesdays, and Thursdays, from ten in the morning until
four in the afternoon, and visitors are advised to write in advance
to confirm their visit, as the room has space for only six people at
a time and is occasionally booked by school groups studying maritime
history. The Skerring collection -- journal, photograph, poem,
Astrid's letters, and a small set of Authority correspondence
relating to the post -- is among the most consulted of the Trust's
holdings, although, as Elena has noted in conversation with at
least three interviewers over the past two decades, the rate of
consultation falls off sharply after the second year following any
new publication or media reference, and what remains, after the
initial flurry, is the steady, slow, almost private interest of
people for whom the keeper's life answers a question they had not
quite known how to ask.

In the spring of 2014, on the centenary of the vacancy that
Halvarsen had filled in 1915, the Trust held a small commemorative
event at the lighthouse itself. Twenty-three people made the
crossing from Tordal in two chartered boats, including a great-
nephew of Marcus Halvarsen from Voss, two great-grandchildren of
Astrid Berg's sister Karen, a retired Authority engineer who had
worked on the 1953 automation, an academic from the University of
Oslo who was writing a book on the cultural history of solitude in
Scandinavia, four members of the Trust's executive committee, a
photographer for a national magazine, and the harbour master of
Tordal, who had succeeded Tomas Lien in the role and who had agreed
to handle the boats himself in honour of the occasion. The weather
was unusually clear. The group walked the perimeter of the island,
visited the foundation of the cottage and the cove where the chest
had been buried, and stood for a few minutes at the base of the
tower while a member of the executive committee read aloud, from a
folder she had brought, the journal entries for the eleventh of
January, 1923, the third of March, 1942, and the eighteenth of
October, 1947. There was no formal speech. After the readings the
group ate a packed lunch on the lee side of the foundation and
returned to Tordal in the late afternoon. The photograph that
accompanied the magazine article a month later showed nine people
seated on the foundation stones, most of them looking not at the
camera but out toward the sea, and one of them -- the great-nephew
from Voss -- holding a small black notebook open on his lap, in
which, he later told the photographer, he had been writing nothing
in particular: only the date, the weather, and the names of the
others present, in a hand that, the photographer noted afterwards
without quite being able to say why, reminded him strongly of the
hand reproduced in the published edition of the journal.

The notebook is now in the Trust's collection, deposited by the
great-nephew in 2019 with a brief note explaining that he had no
particular reason to keep it and that he thought it belonged with
the other Halvarsen papers. It is catalogued under accession
SK-2019-03 and is occasionally consulted, by visitors to the
reading room, alongside the journal itself.
